So. deep breath*

An analog audio signal is just a current generated by a power source whose voltage follows the shape of the original sound wave, meaning it goes up and down really quickly over time just like the sound wave. If you amplify that signal by some 200x, it becomes strong enough to power a laser diode. Since the voltage is going up and down with the sound wave, the resulting brightness of that laser beam will have the audio information “encoded” in it. If you shine that laser beam at a solar panel and then connect the output of that solar panel to a speaker, you’ll hear the original sound. You can then transmit sound like a radio wave through a laser beam across hundreds of feet! The audio quality is pretty awful but it’s very fun to do.

I learned about all that over the summer and found the concept very interesting, and I’m using it as the foundation for a musical “zoetrope”-style instrument. You cut a sound wave out of a strip of opaque plastic and then form that plastic into a ring and place a laser diode in the center. If you shine the laser through a lens that turns the dot into a line, and then shine that through the plastic, the amount of the laser light blocked by the plastic depends on the height (amplitude) of the sound wave at that point. In other words, if you spin the wave around the laser really fast, the amount of laser light passing through will resemble the sound wave right? So if you place a solar panel hooked up to a speaker on the other end, you should hear the sound of the wave printed on the plastic! You can loop through VERY short audio samples like this, the duration depends on the circumference of your plastic ring and how much information you can fit onto it. For reference, the plastic ring I’ve got on my prototype at the moment is roughly 70cm long, and I’m expecting to get maybe half a second of looping audio out of it, optimistically.

Anyway, I’m really excited about eventually having maybe a half-functional prototype that sounds not awful! Hoping to put together some documentation to show everyone how it works as well, as I realize it must sound really confusing on paper. Please pull me aside and ask me about it if you’re curious!

Alan

When you mix Potassium Ferricyanide and Ferric Ammonium Citrate, coat it on an absorbent material, and expose it to the sun, you get an alternative photographic print developed in 1842 by Sir John Herschel called the Cyanotype.

The beautiful thing about this process is that it is extremely forgiving, easy to set up, and you can make a photographic print of anything. For our experiment, we used watercolor paper and found materials, including flowers, dirt, orange peels, sticks, and pieces of paper. Working quickly, we made our collages, covered them with a sheet of glass to prevent the wind from blowing anything away, and let the sun do the rest.

First exposure of 20 mins wasn’t long enough, leaving the prints too dull. Our second exposure of 40 mins was perfect.
